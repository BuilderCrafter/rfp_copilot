# RFP Intake and Bid Qualification Policy

## Purpose

Northstar Digital Solutions uses this policy to decide whether an incoming RFP is worth proposal effort and whether the buyer has provided enough information for a reliable response. This is synthetic demo content for the hackathon knowledge base.

The policy answers two intake questions:

- What do we require from RFPs before we can produce a confident proposal?
- Is this RFP something we want to bid on?

## What We Require From RFPs

Every RFP should contain enough information for a delivery lead, solution architect, commercial owner, and legal reviewer to understand scope, risk, and effort. Missing items do not automatically mean no-bid, but they must become clarification questions before final pricing or contractual commitment.

### Minimum RFP Intake Checklist

1. Business problem and desired outcomes
   - The RFP should describe the buyer problem, target users, business objectives, expected outcomes, and success measures.
   - Missing signal: vague request for a "platform" or "solution" without explaining what operational result the buyer needs.

2. Functional requirements and workflows
   - The RFP should describe core user journeys, roles, permissions, workflow states, exception handling, content needs, notifications, reporting, and administrative features.
   - Missing signal: only high-level goals are provided and no workflow examples are included.

3. Integration landscape and API expectations
   - The RFP should identify source systems, target systems, API availability, authentication expectations, data exchange patterns, integration owners, test environments, and rate or volume constraints.
   - Required documents when available: API documentation, system inventory, architecture diagrams, integration sequence diagrams, sample payloads, and environment access assumptions.

4. Data migration and source data readiness
   - The RFP should explain what legacy data must be migrated, source data quality, data cleansing ownership, retention rules, test data availability, and validation responsibilities.
   - Missing signal: the buyer asks for migration but does not identify systems, record counts, formats, or data quality constraints.

5. Security, privacy, and compliance requirements
   - The RFP should state data classifications, applicable regulations, access-control needs, audit logging, encryption requirements, vulnerability management expectations, incident communication, privacy obligations, and evidence required from the vendor.
   - Required documents when available: security questionnaire, data processing agreement, privacy impact assessment, regulatory control matrix, and cyber insurance requirement.

6. Hosting, operations, support, and continuity
   - The RFP should include hosting preferences, environment requirements, availability targets, monitoring, backup, RTO, RPO, support hours, severity levels, escalation paths, maintenance windows, and release expectations.

7. Procurement and implementation timeline
   - The RFP should include proposal submission deadline, clarification question deadline, award date, expected start date, target launch date, milestone dates, blackout periods, and dependencies that can affect delivery.
   - Missing signal: implementation plan is requested but no buyer timeline or procurement calendar is provided.

8. Budget or affordability guardrails
   - The RFP should provide a budget range, estimated contract value, funding limit, price ceiling, preferred pricing model, or affordability guidance.
   - If no budget is disclosed, the bid manager should ask whether the buyer expects fixed price, time and materials, phased budget release, or capped implementation fees.

9. Evaluation criteria and scoring model
   - The RFP should explain scoring weights, pass/fail requirements, shortlist process, demo expectations, technical versus commercial weighting, and award decision process.
   - Missing signal: the buyer asks for a full proposal but does not describe how responses will be evaluated.

10. Contract terms, liability, and legal review triggers
    - The RFP should include draft contract terms or identify unusual terms such as unlimited liability, uncapped data breach exposure, regulatory penalties, broad indemnities, source-code escrow, or mandatory local-law obligations.
    - Any unlimited liability, uncapped penalty, or mandatory certification claim requires legal review before bid submission.

11. Required attachments and buyer-provided source documents
    - The RFP should list required proposal attachments and provide source documents needed for accurate scoping.
    - Common proposal attachments: project plan, team CVs, data processing agreement, cyber insurance certificate, case studies, architecture diagrams, support model, implementation methodology, and pricing workbook.

12. Acceptance criteria, UAT, and sign-off process
    - The RFP should define acceptance criteria, UAT responsibilities, defect severity rules, sign-off process, production readiness review, and what happens if acceptance is delayed by buyer-side dependencies.

## Is This RFP Something We Want To Bid On?

Northstar generally bids when the opportunity fits our software delivery strengths, target sectors, risk appetite, and commercial model. A good-fit RFP usually has several of these positive signals:

- Custom software, digital service, workflow platform, case-management solution, portal, integration layer, data platform, or cloud-hosted application.
- Buyers in public sector, financial services, healthcare, utilities, education, logistics, or regulated enterprise operations.
- Need for secure integrations, API design, workflow configuration, audit logging, privacy-by-design, accessibility, DevOps, support, and measurable delivery governance.
- Scope can be delivered through discovery, phased implementation, MVP, pilot, or iterative rollout.
- Buyer accepts clarification questions and provides access to business, IT, security, privacy, legal, and operational stakeholders.

## No-Bid and Escalation Triggers

The bid manager should recommend no-bid or executive review when any of these signals appear:

- Civil works, road construction, building construction, renovation, concrete, excavation, structural engineering, HVAC, facilities management, or other construction jobs with no meaningful software scope.
- Hardware-only procurement, equipment supply, device procurement, network cabling, or commodity resale where Northstar is expected to be a reseller rather than a software delivery partner.
- Staff augmentation-only work where the buyer wants contractor resources, time sheets, or body-shopping instead of an accountable delivery team.
- Manufacturing, physical product supply, logistics warehousing, event staffing, or facilities operations without a software platform component.
- Mandatory unlimited liability, uncapped penalties, or acceptance of regulatory fines without negotiated limits.
- Unrealistic delivery timeline such as a complex production platform in two to four weeks without prior discovery or available reusable assets.
- Required certification claims that Northstar cannot currently make, such as ISO 27001 certification or SOC 2 Type II certification, unless a human reviewer verifies the claim.
- Budget is clearly below the likely delivery cost and the buyer does not allow phased scope or discovery.

## Bid Decision Guidance

Use this scoring interpretation for the RFP assessment:

- Bid: strong software/platform fit, no hard no-bid trigger, and missing information can be handled through clarification.
- Needs review: mixed fit, important missing information, legal/commercial risk, compressed timeline, or unclear procurement process.
- No-bid: out-of-scope industry or delivery model, construction or hardware-only work, unacceptable legal risk, or no credible path to a responsible proposal.

## What We May Need To Provide To The Client

The proposal team must prepare a client submission checklist from the RFP. This checklist is different from missing buyer information: it describes documents, evidence, declarations, and internal actions Northstar must provide to the buyer.

Common client submission items include:

- Company profile, company registration extract, ownership declaration, tax clearance, sanctions declaration, conflict-of-interest declaration, and authorized-signatory forms.
- Financial statements, audited accounts, bank reference letter, company credit rating, creditworthiness certificate, and insurance certificates.
- Cyber insurance certificate, professional indemnity insurance, public liability insurance, data processing agreement, privacy addendum, security questionnaire, and incident-response summary.
- Certification evidence or control-alignment statements for ISO 27001, SOC 2, GDPR, WCAG, secure development, vulnerability management, and business continuity. Formal certification claims must be verified before submission.
- Case studies, client references, reference contact details, previous project outcomes, implementation methodology, project plan, delivery timeline, risk register, and governance model.
- Proposed team CVs, role descriptions, availability statements, right-to-work declarations, confidentiality undertakings, and training records.
- Employee criminal background checks, employee credit checks, security clearance evidence, or personnel screening attestations when specifically required by the RFP or buyer due-diligence process.
- Architecture diagrams, integration diagrams, data-flow diagrams, API documentation, support model, SLA table, hosting description, backup and disaster-recovery summary, and monitoring approach.
- Pricing workbook, assumptions and exclusions, license schedule, hosting cost schedule, support-cost schedule, optional enhancements, and third-party cost disclosure.
- Signed RFP forms, compliance matrix, response templates, appendices, mandatory checklists, and any buyer portal upload metadata.

Guidance for sensitive employee checks:

- Criminal background checks, employee credit checks, and security clearance evidence are sensitive HR/legal items. Mark them as required only when the RFP explicitly requests them. Mark them as needs_review when the RFP implies personnel screening but does not specify the method.
- The HR and legal teams must confirm consent, jurisdiction, privacy basis, retention, and disclosure limits before providing employee-level screening evidence.
- Proposal responses should avoid promising blanket employee checks unless the company can perform them legally and operationally for the proposed team.

## Clarification Question Examples

- Please provide the proposal submission deadline, clarification deadline, target award date, and target go-live date.
- Please provide the scoring model, evaluation weights, shortlist process, and demo expectations.
- Please share the integration inventory, API documentation, sample payloads, environments, and integration owners.
- Please confirm the budget range, contract value, pricing model preference, or affordability guardrails.
- Please provide draft contract terms, liability caps, indemnity language, DPA requirements, and insurance expectations.
- Please confirm source data systems, record counts, data formats, data quality constraints, and migration ownership.
- Please provide acceptance criteria, UAT responsibilities, sign-off process, and defect severity rules.
